LabTwin GmbH · Voice AI for Labs · 2023 — 2024

Voice AI for scientists who cannot stop to type.

I helped LabTwin triple deployment frequency from 4 to 12 releases per year while reaching 95% scientific-term speech accuracy for key pharma and biotech customers. As Technical Product Manager at LabTwin, I shipped GPT-3.5-powered intent recognition into the production voice pipeline, turning natural language into lab workflow actions scientists could use hands-free.

Product Voice AI assistant for labs

The problem

The lab was voice-first. The software still expected hands.

In real labs, scientists are not sitting neatly at a desk. They are wearing gloves, handling samples, moving between instruments, and trying to capture observations without breaking flow or contaminating their work.

LabTwin already gave scientists a way to take notes by voice. But the deeper product question was bigger: could voice become a reliable interface for real scientific workflows, inventory updates, data capture, and intent recognition, not just dictation?

My role was to turn that ambition into shippable product work: partner integrations, release discipline, and the company’s first LLM-powered intent recognition system.

3x release frequency increase, from quarterly to monthly shipping

Integration case study

Voice-controlled inventory.

I spent my first weeks shadowing scientists in actual labs. Researchers in protective gear, hands occupied with samples, were stopping mid-experiment to type inventory updates into computers. Every interruption was a contamination risk. Every context switch was a chance for error.

I led the cross-functional effort to extend LabTwin’s voice assistant into inventory management through an integration with Revvity Signals. If a scientist could say, “I used 50ml of sodium chloride,” the system should update inventory automatically.

I coordinated between our engineering team and Revvity’s API team to connect voice commands to their inventory database. Scientists could scan items, check stock levels, and log consumption without touching a keyboard.

  • Scientists could scan and locate items using voice, with real-time location tracking.
  • Inventory records stayed accurate because updates happened in the moment, not hours later from memory.
  • Experimental data and inventory management merged into one workflow.

AI case study

Natural language, not robot language.

Backoffice data showed a clear pattern. Scientists were saying things like “I added around fifty milliliters of the buffer solution,” and the system returned nothing. Regex intent recognition only understood exact command patterns. “Add 50ml” worked. “I just added about fifty” did not.

I championed replacing regex with GPT-3.5 for intent recognition, built the business case, secured stakeholder buy-in, and started with a beta. We tracked false negatives, edge cases, and observation data before moving to production.

The biggest lesson was operational. LLMs need guardrails. They need systematic evaluation, not manual spot checks. This project established practices we could reuse for future AI features.

  • False negatives dropped significantly after switching from regex to GPT-3.5.
  • Scientists could speak naturally instead of memorizing exact command syntax.
  • Established LLM evaluation practices for future AI work.

Operating model

The release itself had become the risk.

Every quarter, the pattern repeated. Three months of building, then two weeks of chaos. Engineering crunched to finish features. QA scrambled to test everything at once. If something broke in production, customers waited months for fixes.

I partnered with the Engineering Lead to move from quarterly to monthly releases. The technical shift was straightforward. The hard part was alignment. QA needed automated regression tests and narrower scope. Customer Success needed lighter release notes and predictable timing. Sales and Marketing still needed quarterly launch themes.

The result was a release train the company could trust. Smaller releases, lower deployment risk, faster customer fixes, and a rhythm everyone could plan around.
